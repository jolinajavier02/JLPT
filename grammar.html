1. The SOV Structure

Unlike English (SVO), Japanese follows a Subject - Object - Verb (SOV) pattern. The verb always comes at the end of the sentence.

English (SVO)

I (S) eat (V) sushi (O).

Japanese (SOV)

Watashi wa (S) sushi o (O) tabemasu (V).

4. Verb Conjugation (Polite)

In polite Japanese, verbs end in -masu. Here is how they change based on tense and positivity.

Form Ending Example (Eat) Meaning
Present Positive -masu Tabemasu I eat / I will eat
Present Negative -masen Tabemasen I do not eat
Past Positive -mashita Tabemashita I ate
Past Negative -masen deshita Tabemasen deshita I did not eat

5. I-Adjectives vs Na-Adjectives

Japanese has two types of adjectives that behave differently when describing nouns or ending sentences.

I-Adjectives (ã„)

End in 'i'. They conjugate like verbs.

Example: Oishii (Delicious)

Oishii desu (It is delicious)

Na-Adjectives (ãª)

Need 'na' to connect to a noun.

Example: Kirei (Beautiful)

Kirei na hana (Beautiful flower)

6. Politeness Levels

Japanese uses different levels of politeness based on who you are talking to.

Polite (Desu/Masu)

Used with strangers, teachers, or coworkers. Safe for almost any situation.

Ikimasu (I go - polite)

Casual (Dictionary Form)

Used with friends and family. This is the form you find in dictionaries.

Iku (I go - casual)
